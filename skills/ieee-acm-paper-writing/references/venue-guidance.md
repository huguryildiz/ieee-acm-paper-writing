# IEEE and ACM venue guidance

Use this reference for venue adaptation. Verify the exact publication, article type, current author
instructions, and official template before applying any rule.

## Contents

- Shared verification contract
- IEEE-oriented checks
- ACM-oriented checks
- Submission and disclosure ledger

## Shared verification contract

Record publication name, article type, official instruction URL, template/version, access date,
length policy, review layout, anonymization, bibliography style, supplementary-material rules,
artifact policy, accessibility requirements, ethics declarations, and generative-AI disclosure.

Never infer that all publications under one publisher share page limits, section order, review
format, author visibility, bibliography details, or disclosure policy. Mark an unverified rule as
`unverified venue rule` in the handoff, not in manuscript prose.

Formatting authority comes from current official instructions and templates. Scientific authority
comes from verified evidence. A template cannot justify a scientific claim, and a published paper
cannot substitute for a current submission rule.

## IEEE-oriented checks

- Identify the exact journal, Transactions title, magazine, letter, or conference.
- Use the official template and author center linked by that venue.
- Verify whether initial submission requires single-column, double-column, or source files.
- Verify abstract length, index terms, graphical abstract, highlights, biographies, and page-charge
  policies rather than importing them from another IEEE title.
- When index terms are required but their count or ordering is unspecified, select a concise set of
  domain-specific noun phrases from the manuscript's verified terminology. Treat the count and
  ordering as editorial choices, not as venue requirements.
- Verify review anonymity and whether acknowledgments, repository links, and self-citations require
  masking.
- Verify reference style details, early-access treatment, datasets/software citations, and
  supplementary-file naming.
- Verify figure formats, resolution, fonts, color accessibility, and permission requirements.
- Verify ORCID, funding, conflicts, human/animal-subject declarations, data availability, and AI
  disclosure requirements for the specific venue.

Preserve mathematical meaning and cross-references during template conversion. Do not compress
assumptions, comparators, uncertainty, or limitations merely to meet a page target; instead identify
what content can move to permitted supplementary material without hiding load-bearing evidence.

### IEEE editorial style essentials

The following defaults derive from the IEEE Editorial Style Manual for Authors. Apply them when
adapting or auditing an IEEE manuscript; the current edition of the manual and venue-specific
author instructions override this summary.

Text mechanics:

- Use American spelling per the first Merriam-Webster main-entry spelling ("behavior," not
  "behaviour"). Do not use contractions in technical text.
- Define an acronym at its first use in the abstract and again at its first use in the body.
  Acronym and decade plurals take no apostrophe (FETs, 1990s).
- In titles, capitalize nouns, pronouns, adjectives, verbs, adverbs, and subordinating
  conjunctions; lowercase articles, coordinating conjunctions, and short prepositions unless first
  or last. In running text, capitalize only proper names attached to laws, theorems, and
  principles (Abel's theorem).
- Use the international date format with the month spelled out (4 June 2002).
- Use numerals for numbers with units; otherwise spell out numbers below 11. Write percentages as
  numeral plus % and repeat the symbol in ranges and lists (20%–30%). Use an en dash for ranges
  ("to," "through") and give the unit only after the final number when units match (40–50 mm).
- Use "that" for restrictive clauses and "which," set off by commas, for nonrestrictive clauses.
  Use the serial comma before the coordinating conjunction. Place punctuation inside quotation
  marks except colons and semicolons.
- Do not hyphenate predicate adjectives ("is well known") or compound modifiers that follow the
  noun; keep hyphenation of any given term consistent throughout the article. Do not use ™ or ®
  symbols.

Structure and apparatus:

- Abstract: one paragraph of roughly 150-250 words with no citations, numbered equations, or
  footnotes. Index terms follow as the final paragraph of the abstract, in alphabetical order,
  first word capitalized, acronyms defined.
- Number equations consecutively through the article. Refer to an equation in text as "in (1)";
  write out "Equation (1)" only at the start of a sentence.
- Use one reference per number; grouping several references under one number is not permitted.
- Cite figures and tables in numerical order at first mention. Abbreviate "Fig." even at the start
  of a sentence; number tables with Roman numerals and place titles above them.
- Use "Appendixes" as the plural. Place the Acknowledgment (singular, no "e" before "-ment")
  after the appendixes and before the references; place a conflict-of-interest section before the
  Acknowledgment. Disclose AI-generated content where the venue directs, typically in the
  Acknowledgment.
- Heading hierarchy: primary headings numbered with Roman numerals, secondary with capital
  letters, tertiary with Arabic numerals, quaternary with lowercase letters.
- In biographies and any reference to a person, defer to the pronoun or title the author provides.
- Use people-first, inclusive language per the IEEE inclusive-language guidance.

### IEEE reference style essentials

The following defaults derive from the IEEE Reference Style Guide for Authors (IEEE Publication
Operations). Apply them when building or auditing an IEEE reference list; the current edition of
the guide and venue-specific author instructions override this summary.

In-text citations:

- Cite on the line, in square brackets, inside the punctuation. Citations may read as nouns:
  "according to [1]," "Brown and Jones [5]," "Wood et al. [7]."
- Write every cited number out; do not use en-dash ranges: "[1], [2], [3], [4]," never "[1]–[4]."
- Use "et al." in text when a cited work has three or more authors.
- Cite parts of a work as "[3, Thm. 1]," "[3, pp. 5–10]," "[3, eq. (2)]," "[3, Fig. 1],"
  "[3, Sect. 4.5]," "[3, Ch. 2, pp. 5–10]," "[3, Algorithm 5]." Replace "ibid." and "op. cit."
  with the explicit earlier reference number and renumber accordingly.

Reference-list mechanics:

- Reference numbers sit flush left in square brackets, forming their own hanging column.
- Give authors' names as initials preceding the surname; no commas around Jr., Sr., or III.
- List up to six author names; for more than six, give the first author followed by "et al."
  For non-IEEE sources, "et al." is also acceptable when further names are unavailable.
- End every reference with a period unless it ends with a URL. When a reference carries both a
  DOI or accessed date and a URL, place the DOI or accessed date first, close it with a period,
  and end with the URL without a final period.
- Every reference needs at least a year of publication or access; when no date exists, put
  "(n.d.)" in the date position.
- Separate two months of one issue with a slash (Jul./Aug.).

Canonical formats for common source types:

- Journal article: J. K. Author, "Name of paper," Abbrev. Title of Periodical, vol. x, no. x,
  pp. xxx–xxx, Abbrev. Month, year, doi: xxx. Use "Art. no. xxx" in place of the page range when
  the venue assigns article IDs; a lone long "page number" or a non-sequential range usually
  signals a mislabeled article ID.
- Early access: append "early access," the date of publication, and the DOI. Cite the
  publisher's early-access version rather than the arXiv version; the DOI is essential because it
  will not change. Use "to be published" for accepted but unpublished work and "submitted for
  publication" before acceptance; never "to appear in."
- Conference paper (presentation only): J. K. Author, "Title of paper," presented at the Abbrev.
  Name of Conf., City, State, Country, Month and day(s), year, Paper number.
- Proceedings paper: J. K. Author, "Title of paper," in Abbrev. Name of Conf., year,
  pp. xxx–xxx, doi: xxx. The location is optional; every published proceedings paper has page
  numbers; the trailing year may be dropped when it already appears in the conference title.
- Book or chapter: J. K. Author, "Title of chapter," in Title of Published Book, X. Editor, Ed.,
  xth ed. City, State (U.S. only), Country: Abbrev. of Publisher, year, ch. x, sect. x,
  pp. xxx–xxx. Mark untranslated foreign publications "(in Language)" after the publisher;
  credit translations as "trans. J. K. Translator."
- Technical report: J. K. Author, "Title of report," Abbrev. Name of Co., City, State, Country,
  Rep. xxx, year.
- Thesis or dissertation: J. K. Author, "Title," M.S. thesis or Ph.D. dissertation, Abbrev.
  Dept., Abbrev. Univ., City, State, Country, year. Keep the author's own "thesis" or
  "dissertation" label; omit the state when the university name contains it.
- Standard: Title of Standard, Standard number, corporate author and location if given, date.
- Patent: J. K. Author, "Title of patent," U.S. Patent x xxx xxx, Abbrev. Month, day, year.
  Retain the day; use the issued date when several dates are given.
- arXiv preprint: J. K. Author, "Title of paper," year, arXiv number.
- Dataset: Author, Date, "Title of Dataset," Source, doi: xxx — or end with
  ". [Online]. Available: URL" when no DOI exists.
- Software: Author. Title of Software. (Date). Repository or archive. (Version or year).
  Publisher. Accessed date when applicable. [Online]. Persistent identifier or URL.
- Website: J. K. Author. "Page Title." Website Title. Accessed: Abbrev. Month day, year.
  [Online]. Available: URL
- Unpublished: "private communication, Abbrev. Month, year" or J. K. Author, "Title of paper,"
  unpublished.

Datasets and software follow the FORCE11 citation principles: cite them as first-class research
products wherever a claim relies on them, with a persistent, machine-actionable identifier and
enough version and provenance detail to retrieve the exact data or software used.

Abbreviations and titles:

- Abbreviate conference-name words per the IEEE list (Proc., Rec., Int., Conf., Symp., Annu.,
  Colloq., Congr., Conv., Expo., Nat., Dig., Tech. Dig.), write ordinals as numerals (1st, 2nd),
  and omit most articles and prepositions such as "of the" and "on."
- Abbreviate periodical titles per the IEEE abbreviation lists; never abbreviate a one-word
  title (Science, Nature). Words ending in "-ology" truncate after "-ol." and "-graphy" after
  "-gr."; compound words end with the final word's abbreviation; spell out any word without a
  listed abbreviation.
- Before 1988, IEEE Transactions volume numbers carry the journal acronym (vol. AC-26);
  Proceedings of the IEEE never carried one. Verify IEEE Transactions issue numbers and months
  on IEEE Xplore — not every title has them; articles published within a volume use the volume
  and issue publication date.

Online references:

- Accessed-date form: "Accessed: Abbrev. Month day, year."
- Break URLs after a slash, double slash, or period, and before a hyphen, tilde, underscore,
  question mark, or percent sign; never insert hyphens or let an address hyphenate.

## ACM-oriented checks

- Identify the exact journal, Transactions title, proceedings series, or conference track.
- Use the official ACM template mode and venue-specific submission instructions.
- Verify review anonymity, rights-management stage, author-year versus numeric citation mode, CCS
  concepts, keywords, ACM reference format, and artifact badges.
- Distinguish review manuscript settings from camera-ready production settings.
- Verify accessibility requirements for figures, alternative text, tables, and color.
- Verify page or word limits, appendices, supplementary material, artifact evaluation, data/code
  availability, ethics review, conflicts, funding, and AI disclosure.
- Verify ORCID: ACM requires every author to register an ORCID iD to complete the publishing
  workflow for an accepted article.
- Verify the rights and access model rather than assuming it. ACM has moved to a fully
  open-access model; confirm whether the article is covered under an institutional agreement
  (e.g. ACM Open) or carries an article-processing charge, and treat this as an author obligation
  to resolve, not a formatting detail.
- Where generative-AI use must be disclosed, verify whether the disclosure statement itself must
  sit inside the anonymized submission for blind review; do not let the disclosure leak author
  identity, and never list an AI tool as an author.
- Do not manually reproduce production metadata or rights blocks before the workflow requires them.

When adapting LaTeX, preserve semantic structure and use the official class commands rather than
visual formatting hacks. Confirm that anonymization does not leak through PDF metadata, artifact
URLs, acknowledgments, or repository history where the venue requires blind review.

## Submission and disclosure ledger

Before declaring compliance, record:

1. exact venue and article/track type;
2. official sources and access date;
3. template and compilation mode;
4. length and supplementary-material treatment;
5. anonymity and metadata checks;
6. bibliography and citation requirements;
7. figures, tables, accessibility, and permissions;
8. artifact, data, and code declarations;
9. ethics, funding, conflicts, and author identifiers;
10. generative-AI policy and required disclosure;
11. unresolved or contradictory rules.

Return adapted text with verified requirements, unresolved requirements, and scientific content
intentionally left unchanged.
