# IEEE Editorial Style Manual for Authors — rules digest

Repo-side documentation; not installed with the skill. This digest records the editorial rules
from the *IEEE Editorial Style Manual for Authors* (IEEE Publication Operations) that inform the
skill, so their provenance survives independently of the source document.

- Source: IEEE Author Center, *IEEE Editorial Style Manual for Authors*
  (https://journals.ieeeauthorcenter.ieee.org/). Accessed 14 July 2026 via a published copy
  supplied by the maintainer.
- Encoded in the skill: "IEEE editorial style essentials" in
  [`venue-guidance.md`](../../skills/ieee-acm-paper-writing/references/venue-guidance.md).
- Precedence: the current edition of the manual and venue-specific author instructions override
  everything below.

## Front matter

- **Title capitalization.** Capitalize nouns, pronouns, adjectives, verbs, adverbs, and
  subordinating conjunctions; lowercase articles, coordinating conjunctions, and most short
  prepositions unless first or last word.
- **Author bylines.** Longest, most complete names; no nicknames or maiden names in the byline
  (permitted parenthetically in the biography); no comma before Jr./Sr.
- **First footnote.** Three paragraphs: (1) received/revised/accepted and online publication
  dates; (2) author affiliations with country and email; (3) supplementary-material notice with
  DOI link.
- **Abstract.** One paragraph, 150–250 words; no numbered equations, citations, or footnotes;
  variables lightface italic, numbers and units bold (per the journal's abstract styling).
- **Index terms.** Alphabetical order, as the final paragraph of the Abstract section; first word
  capitalized; acronyms defined.
- **Nomenclature.** Symbol list with definitions; items end with periods; acronyms defined in the
  Nomenclature need no redefinition in text.

## Body structure

- **Section headings, four levels.** Primary: Roman numerals, centered, small caps. Secondary:
  capital letters, flush left, italic. Tertiary: Arabic numerals, indented, run into text,
  italic. Quaternary: lowercase letters, indented further, only first letter capitalized.
- **Introduction.** Full-length articles open with a drop cap (unless the text begins with a
  quotation mark).
- **Equation numbering.** Consecutive from beginning to end of the article; numbers flush right
  on the last line of the equation. In text, refer to "(1)"; write "Equation (1)" only at the
  start of a sentence.
- **Appendix.** Plural is "Appendixes," not "Appendices." Appendix figures and tables continue
  the main article's numbering.
- **Conflict of interest.** Its own section at the end of the article, before the Acknowledgment.
- **Acknowledgment.** After the final text and appendixes, just before References. Spelled
  singular, with no "e" between "g" and "m." AI-generated content is disclosed here.
- **References.** One reference per number; grouping several references under one number is not
  permitted.
- **Figures and tables.** First citations must appear in numerical order. "Fig." is abbreviated
  even at the start of a sentence. Tables are numbered with Roman numerals, titles centered above.
  Placement: tops of columns, as close after first mention as possible; progression is vertical
  on the page. Footnotes sit at the bottom of the column where first mentioned.
- **Biographies.** Generally three paragraphs; defer to the pronoun or title the author provides;
  IEEE membership grade abbreviations included; current photographs encouraged.

## Writing style

- **Acronyms.** Define at first use in the Abstract and again at first use in the body. Plurals
  take no apostrophe (FETs, 1990s).
- **Spelling.** First spelling of the main entry in Merriam-Webster; American spellings replace
  British ones ("behaviour" → "behavior").
- **Trademarks.** ™ and ® symbols are not used; capitalize only the first letter of the name.
- **Contractions.** Not used in technical text ("don't care," "best-case," "worst-case" excepted
  in some journals).
- **Capitalization in text.** Discouraged except where necessary; only proper names attached to
  laws, principles, and theorems are capitalized (Abel's theorem).
- **Dates.** International format with the month spelled out: 4 June 2002.
- **Percentages.** Numeral plus % sign; the symbol repeats in lists and ranges (20%–30%).
- **Decimals.** Include a leading zero only when needed for clarity; no trailing zeros after the
  last significant digit.
- **Ranges with units.** Unit only after the final number unless the units differ (40–50 mm;
  50 inches to 7 feet).
- **Numbers.** Numerals for numbers with units; otherwise spell out numbers below 11.
- **En dash vs. em dash.** En dash means "to," "through," or "and" in ranges and opposites; em
  dash marks a suspension of the sense in ordinary prose.
- **Hyphenation.** Do not hyphenate most compound modifiers after the noun, predicate adjectives
  ("is well known"), or comparatives/superlatives ("higher order equation"). Above all, keep each
  term's hyphenation consistent within the article.

## Grammar and punctuation

- **Possessives.** Singular nouns add 's (Avogadro's theorem); words ending in s take the
  apostrophe alone (Burns' theorem); possessive pronouns take no apostrophe.
- **Series.** Serial comma before the coordinating conjunction in series of three or more;
  semicolons separate displayed list items.
- **Clauses.** Restrictive clauses use "that"; nonrestrictive clauses use "which" and are set off
  by commas.
- **Semicolons and colons.** Semicolon links independent clauses (alone or with "however,"
  "therefore," "nevertheless"); colon follows an independent clause to introduce a list.
- **Quotation marks.** Punctuation goes inside quotation marks, except colons and semicolons;
  direct quotes in roman font.
- **Compound nouns and verbs.** One-syllable-verb + adverb compounds are one word when the
  dictionary lists them so (setup, takeoff); compound verbs are generally hyphenated (arc-weld).

## Special article types

- **Editorials.** No Abstract; Acknowledgment without heading; author name as closing signature.
- **Brief/short papers, letters, correspondence.** Formatted like full articles, without
  biographies or photographs; short forms omit membership grade.
- **Comments and replies.** First sentence begins "In the above article [1]," with the referenced
  article as [1]; Abstract and Index Terms included.
- **Corrections/errata.** Plural form in the title even for a single correction; byline matches
  the original article exactly.

## Inclusive language

Use people-first language ("person who has X") and the IEEE inclusive-language guide's
replacement terms for insensitive terminology across racial, disability, and gender-related
categories.
